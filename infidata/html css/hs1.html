INDIAN SOLDIERS


  Captain Vikram Batra

    Captain Vikram Batra, PVC (9 September 1974 – 7 July 1999) was an officer     of the Indian Army, posthumously awarded with the Param Vir Chakra, India's     highest and most prestigious award for valour, for his actions during the 1999     Kargil War in Kashmir between India and Pakistan. He led one of the toughest     operations in mountain warfare in Indian history. He was often referred to as     ‘'Sher Shah'’ ("Lion King") in the intercepted messages of the Pakistan Army.





  Ian Cardozo

    Ian Cardozo, AVSM, SM is a former Indian Army General. He was the first     war-disabled officer of Indian Army to command a battalion and a brigade.[4]     He is an amputee due to a war injury.[During the Bangladesh Liberation War,     he stepped on a land mine and his leg was critically injured. Due to non     availability of morphine or pethidine, and absence of medics, his leg could not     be amputated surgically. He subsequently used a khukri to cut his own leg.





  Yogendra Singh Yadav

    Yogendra Singh Yadav PVC is a Junior Commissioned Officer (JCO) of the     Indian Army, who was awarded the highest Indian military honour, the Param     Vir Chakra, for his 4 July 1999 action during the Kargil War. Aged 19 when he     received the decoration, he is recorded as the youngest person to ever be     awarded the Param Vir Chakra.





  Somnath Sharma

    Somnath Sharma, PVC (31 January 1923 – 3 November 1947), an Indian Army     officer, was the first recipient of Param Vir Chakra (PVC), India's highest military     decoration.[2] In 1942, Sharma was commissioned into the 8th Battalion, 19th     Hyderabad Regiment. He served in Burma during the Arakan Campaign of World     War II, for which he was mentioned in despatches.He later fought in the Indo     Pakistani War of 1947. Sharma was killed on 3 November 1947 while evicting     Pakistani infiltrators from Srinagar Airport, and was posthumously awarded the     Param Vir Chakra for his actions prior to his death.
